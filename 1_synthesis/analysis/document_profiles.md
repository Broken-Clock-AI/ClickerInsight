### **Document Profile: BusyDoingNothingWhatDoPlayersDoInIdleGames.md**

*   **Abstract/Executive Summary:** This academic paper investigates player engagement in "idle games," using the popular mobile game *Neko Atsume* as a case study. Through a large survey (N=1972), the authors found that players are highly engaged, but not in ways that fit traditional models of game engagement (like immersion or flow). Player engagement in *Neko Atsume* manifests in four distinct ways: Time spent playing, Direct sociability, Social media sociability, and Checking frequency. The authors propose that engagement in idle games could be better understood as a habit that players acquire and maintain, rather than a series of intense play sessions.

*   **Abstract/Executive Summary:** This bachelor's thesis investigates the qualities that make interactions in "incremental idle clickers" compulsive and motivating. The author explores the tension between interactivity (active clicking) and interpassivity (letting the game play itself). ### **Document Profile: Disengagement_From_Games_Alexandrovsky_et_al_2024.md**

*   **Abstract/Executive Summary:** This paper argues that "disengagement" from games—the process of exiting a play session—should be a constructively designed part of the player experience. Current research focuses heavily on engagement, often treating disengagement only in the context of problematic play. Through an interview study (N=16) and a follow-up online survey (N=111), the authors explore how players experience the end of a play session. They find that positive exit experiences are associated with a sense of satisfaction and closure, while negative experiences involve frustration and regret. The research characterizes disengagement as a complex, multi-step process that players approach strategically, influenced by the game's structure, the player's state, and external factors. The authors conclude with design reflections, emphasizing the need to value temporary disengagement, account for player affect, and prioritize player agency to create games that don't trap players.

*   **Abstract/Executive Summary:** This paper examines idle games (also called incremental, passive, or self-playing games) through the lens of "interpassivity," a concept from Robert Pfaller and Slavoj Žižek describing delegated enjoyment.
