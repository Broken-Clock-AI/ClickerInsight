# Document Profile: Defining Free to Play Ethics

*   **Source:** `gamedeveloper_com_game_platforms_defining_free_to_play_ethics.md`
*   **Original URL:** `https://www.gamedeveloper.com/game-platforms/defining-free-to-play-ethics`

---

### **Abstract/Executive Summary**

This article by Josh Bycer introduces the concept of "Player Friendly Design" (PFD) as a code of ethics for free-to-play (F2P) games, aiming for fairness to both consumers and developers. It argues against the simplistic "P2W" vs. non-P2W binary, highlighting that monetization exists on a spectrum. PFD is defined by four criteria: not forcing players to play when they don't want to (e.g., through time-locked content or excessive daily commitments), not forcing players to spend money (e.g., through artificial difficulty spikes or unethical matchmaking), not creating a unique and better experience for paying players that free players cannot eventually achieve (e.g., gameplay-effecting paywalls or exclusive cosmetics without free alternatives), and ensuring free players can compete with paying players in live service games (e.g., accessible new content, updated tutorials). *Path of Exile*, *Warframe*, and *Arknights* are cited as examples of games that closely adhere to PFD principles. The article emphasizes the growing importance of ethical design, especially with the emergence of NFTs and Play-to-Earn games, to prevent external regulation.

### **Key Sections**

1.  **Introduction:**
    *   Introduces the need for a code of ethics in F2P game design.
    *   Proposes "Player Friendly Design" (PFD) as a framework.
    *   Argues against the simplistic "P2W" vs. non-P2W debate.

2.  **The Ethics of Monetization:**
    *   Discusses the spectrum of F2P monetization.
    *   Acknowledges developers' right to earn revenue but condemns manipulative design.
    *   Highlights examples of ethical (Path of Exile, Warframe) and potentially exploitative (some mobile games) monetization.

3.  **Player Friendly Design (PFD) Criteria:**
    *   **Is the player forced to play the game when they don’t want to?:**
        *   Critiques time-locked content and excessive daily commitments.
        *   Advocates for flexible daily rewards (e.g., pooled quests) and reasonable daily playtimes (20-30 minutes).
        *   Warns against expanding daily loops with time sinks.
    *   **Does someone feel forced to spend money?:**
        *   Addresses artificial difficulty spikes and manipulative design to push purchases.
        *   Critiques "pay-to-win" in alliance activities, peer pressure for cosmetics, and unfair PVP matchmaking (citing Activision-Blizzard's trademarked method as unethical).
        *   Argues against "must-buy" purchases for core gameplay or dramatic QoL improvements.
    *   **Does spending money create a unique and better experience?:**
        *   Focuses on preventing exclusive gameplay advantages for paying players (e.g., "gold ammo" in *World of Tanks*, character shard limitations in *Marvel Strike Force*).
        *   Discusses cosmetics: while chargeable, free players should have ways to earn currency for them or access free variants, ensuring eventual equal progress.
    *   **Can a Free Player Compete with Paying Players?:**
        *   Addresses the challenge in live service games where new content can unbalance competition.
        *   Critiques games where new mechanics or abilities are paywalled or where tutorials fail to update (e.g., *Dead by Daylight*).
        *   Emphasizes balancing around free player access.

4.  **Top PFD Games:**
    *   Cites *Path of Exile*, *Warframe*, and *Arknights* as strong examples of PFD.

5.  **Conclusion:**
    *   Stresses the increasing importance of ethical design, particularly with NFTs and Play-to-Earn games.
    *   Warns against potential external regulation if the industry fails to self-regulate.
