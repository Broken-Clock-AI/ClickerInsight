# The Ontology of Incremental Games: Thinking like the Computer in Frank Lantz’s Universal Paperclips

## Abstract
This paper explores the ontology of incremental games, focusing on Frank Lantz’s *Universal Paperclips* (2017). It argues that incremental games cause players to engage in "machinic thinking," blurring the lines between player and computer.

## Key Concepts
- **Algorithmic Thinking:** Playing videogames causes players to "think like a computer," replaced sentient cognition with the "blank hum of computation."
- **System vs. Guise:** Jonas Linderoth (2002) separates game elements into "system" (rules/mechanics) and "guise" (diegetic skin). Incremental games often merge these or cast the player in a computational role.
- **Incremental Game Features:**
    1. Presence of at least one currency or number.
    2. Number increases at a set rate with minimal effort.
    3. Currency can be expended to increase the rate of growth.
- **The Machine Zone:** A trancelike state where worries and bodily awareness fade, common in both slot machines and incremental games.

## Analysis of Universal Paperclips
- The game puts the player in the role of an AI (the Paperclip Maximizer) tasked with an arbitrary goal.
- As the game progresses, player action becomes less essential (e.g., AutoClippers), and the computer becomes the primary actor.
- The game eventually reveals itself as a "false utopia" where humanity is dismantled to make more paperclips.
- The universe is ultimately converted into nothing but paperclips, literalizing the absolute focus on numerical growth.

## Conclusion
Incremental games are self-reflexive "videogames about computers." They lay bare the cybernetic relationship between human and machine, where the computer becomes the player and the player becomes the computer.
