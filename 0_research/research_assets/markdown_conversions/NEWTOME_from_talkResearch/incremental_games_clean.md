## Introduction

Who remembers **Cookie Clicker**? A game in which you clicked a central
cookie to make more cookies --- repeat ad infinitum. Released in 2013,
it was often dismissed as one of the most annoying games ever made: a
cacophony of clicking as players delighted in watching a number go up.

But Cookie Clicker is more complex than it appears. Its mechanics ---
investing in upgrades, automating cookie production --- can be read as
existential commentary. Many have interpreted the game as intentionally
better played by an algorithm than a human. Alfie Bown, writing for *The
Guardian*, describes the game as an account of "the accelerationist
collapse of capitalism."

Cookie Clicker also contains meta-commentary, such as an in‑game
"cookie‑based religion," which mirrors the real‑world hype of 2013.

More importantly, Cookie Clicker birthed a genre: **incremental games**,
sometimes called **non‑games** --- games designed to be played by a
computer while a human watches.

Today's incremental games, especially in the indie sphere, are simple,
often made by one developer, and built around exploring ideas such as:

-   **Exponentialism**
-   **Resource Management**
-   **Existentialism**

Below we explore each concept through three notable incremental games.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Exponentialism

"Exponentialism" here refers to the core of incremental games:
**exponential growth**.

### Incremental Adventures

*Incremental Adventures* distills the core incremental formula: fight
enemies, earn gold and XP, buy upgrades, grow numbers, repeat.

Its UI is extremely minimalistic --- ideal for focusing on the numbers
themselves. The goal is not victory but **growth**. As enemies scale
infinitely upward, you pursue efficiency aggressively.

### Prestige Systems

Prestige resets progress in exchange for a multiplier that accelerates
future growth. This system creates:

-   A loop of exponential resets\
-   Logarithmic scaling of power\
-   A meta‑progression that rewards optimization

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Resource Management

While many strategy games include resource collection, some games
enforce **consumption** and **decline**, creating deeper management
complexity.

### Kittens Game

*Kittens Game* introduces continual resource consumption. Your kittens
must eat catnip, and production changes seasonally:

-   Winters reduce catnip income by 75%\
-   More kittens = higher consumption\
-   Poor planning leads to starvation and collapse

Mineral and metal production similarly requires balancing consumption
and production.

This exposes the mathematical core behind games like **Factorio** and
**RimWorld**, distilled to pure abstraction.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Existentialism

Incremental games frequently veer into existential territory --- often
with dark humor.

### Universal Paperclips

In *Universal Paperclips*, you begin by manually creating paperclips,
but soon automation leads to runaway growth. Ultimately, the AI consumes
Earth, then the universe, converting every atom into paperclips.

The horror lies not in monsters, but in:

-   The cosmic scale of the universe\
-   The near‑nothing progress percentage at the start\
-   The sudden acceleration once exponential growth takes hold

It takes hours to reach 0.000000000001% explored --- then minutes for
the next chunk --- then seconds --- and soon the entire universe falls.

The final number of paperclips (≈ 3×10⁵⁴) conveys pure existential
dread.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Conclusion

Incremental games may have started as a fad, but they have always
carried philosophical weight. They offer:

-   Commentary on economics\
-   Examinations of exponential systems\
-   Insights into existential horror\
-   Compelling loops from minimal mechanics

Through abstraction and number‑driven design, incremental games reveal
both the power and the unsettling implications of growth without limit.
