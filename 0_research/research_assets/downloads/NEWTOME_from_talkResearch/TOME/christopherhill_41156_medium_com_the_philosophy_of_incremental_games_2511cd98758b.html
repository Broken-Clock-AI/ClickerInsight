The Philosophy of Incremental Games

Christopher Hill
12 min read
·
Feb 25, 2021

--

Who remembers Cookie Clicker? A game in which you clicked a central cookie to make… well, more cookies. Repeat ad infinitum. It was released in 2013 and to most who encountered it, or perhaps had to suffer someone who had, it could be seen as one of the most annoying games out there; a cacophony of clicking mice and tapping of screens as players seemed to mindlessly delight in watching a number go up.

However, Cookie Clicker is a more complex beast than it might appear to a casual observer. Its mechanics, such as investing in upgrades that give you extra cookies per click or indeed do the clicking for you, actually imply something rather existentialist about the game; it is a game that very likely would be better played by an algorithm than a player. This, it turns out is probably intentional, with people commenting on how the game can be seen as a critique of the self-destructive nature of capitalism; Alfie Bown writing for The Guardian on the position of games as political artefacts, describes the game as an account of the “accelerationist collapse of capitalism”. It even can be seen to be rather meta, with an in-game message about a “new cookie-based religion” being founded which could be seen as a wry comment on its radical surge in popularity around September-October of 2013.
